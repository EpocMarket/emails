Chronic Cough
 
Anne Meneghetti, MD
 
Cough is the most common presenting symptom in primary care practice. Subacute cough is defined as 3 to 8 weeks of persistent cough; chronic cough persists for more than 8 weeks. Common causes of subacute cough include postinfectious cough, bronchitis, and pertussis. Chronic cough in nonsmoking adults with a normal chest x-ray, not on ACE inhibitors, is typically caused by one or more of these 4 conditions: upper airway cough syndrome (formerly known as postnasal drip), asthma, GERD, or non-asthmatic eosinophilic bronchitis.

The presence of dyspnea, hemoptysis, weight loss, fever, night sweats, chest pain, or immunocompromise should prompt an aggressive work-up. Non-targeted cough suppressant therapy is rarely effective for chronic cough.

MedWatch Safety Alert for Ampyra
The FDA issued a safety announcement for the multiple sclerosis drug, Ampyra (dalfampridine). Seizures are a known side effect of Ampyra, with increased risk associated with higher blood levels of the drug.

Ampyra is eliminated via the kidneys, and should be used with caution in patients with mild renal impairment (CrCl 51-80 mL/min) and avoided in patients with moderate to severe impairment (CrCl less than or equal to 50 mL/min).

Renal function should be evaluated prior to initiating treatment and at least annually while on the drug. Patients with a history of seizures should not take Ampyra.

Patients should be instructed to take the tablet whole (do not cut/crush/chew/dissolve) and to avoid taking double or extra doses if a dose is missed.

New Generic Drug Monographs
Four new first-time generic formulations recently launched:

1) the nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor, abacavir (generic for Ziagen), to treat HIV infection in adults and children;

2) the second generation antihistamine, desloratadine (generic for Clarinex), to treat allergic rhinitis in adults and children;

3) the combination atypical antipsychotic/selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor, olanzapine/fluoxetine (generic for Symbyax), to treat depressive episodes associated with bipolar I disorder and treatment resistant major depressive disorder in adults; and

4) the muscarinic receptor antagonist, tolterodine (generic for Detrol), to treat overactive bladder in adults.
