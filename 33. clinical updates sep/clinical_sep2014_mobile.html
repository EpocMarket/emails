Brucellosis
 
Walt Hadikin, MD
 
Human brucellosis is one of the most common zoonotic diseases worldwide, with an incidence exceeding 10 per 100,000 in certain countries, particularly in poor rural settings. Most cases in North America are acquired overseas and/or from consumption of unpasteurized milk products including cheese. The four main pathogens causing human disease are Brucella melitensis (goats, camels), B. abortus (cattle), B. suis (pigs), and B. canis (dogs). Considered a class B bioterrorism agent, the disease is easily spread by aerosol and poses a significant hazard in microbiology labs.

Brucellosis one of the “great imitators” in medicine, as it can affect any organ system, resulting in a variable presentation. Fever of unknown origin with associated rheumatic features is present in about 50% of cases. Diagnosis is based on history and lab investigations including blood culture and serological tests. Uncomplicated disease is primarily treated with dual antibiotics (e.g., oral doxycycline + a parenteral aminoglycoside).

Tramadol Declared Schedule IV Drug
The DEA designated the synthetic opioid analgesic tramadol a Schedule IV substance, effective August 18, 2014. Its opioid activity and abuse potential are attributable to both the parent compound and a highly potent active metabolite, M1. Tramadol was previously an uncontrolled substance at the federal level, but its status varied by state, with multiple states already scheduling it as a C-IV drug.

Zydelig Approved for Three Types of Blood Cancers
The FDA approved Zydelig (idelalisib) for the treatment of three types of relapsed blood cancers: chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), follicular B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma (FL), and small lymphocytic lymphoma (SLL). Zydelig is an oral phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K) inhibitor and has a Boxed Warning for hepatotoxicity, severe diarrhea or colitis, pneumonitis, and intestinal perforation.

Beleodaq Approved for Relapsed or Refractory Peripheral T-cell Lymphoma
The FDA recently granted accelerated approval to the orphan drug Beleodaq (belinostat). Belinostat is a new histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor for the treatment of relapsed or refractory peripheral T-cell lymphoma (PTCL), a rare type of non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL). It is given as an IV infusion once daily for 5 days each cycle.
