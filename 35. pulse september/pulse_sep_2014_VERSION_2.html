I have empathy for groups of expert clinicians who sequester themselves in hotel conference rooms for days to create a document that's part evidence-based analysis and part expert opinion. In the end, the resulting clinical practice guideline may bear little semblance to what any of those authors do in practice. Freshly minted guidelines are typically super lengthy, are hard to apply to complex patients, and, annoyingly, opinions can differ from one specialty society to the next. Increasingly, when evidence is lacking, guidelines leave decision-making up to clinician and patient preference. Yet, Epocrates members love—and need—clinical practice guidelines more than ever.
 
Bringing CPGs to Point of Care
There's growing evidence that adhering to clinical practice guidelines can improve care outcomes. The challenge has become distilling relevant guideline-based insights for moment-of-care decision-making. Take the example of atrial fibrillation, one of the top conditions seen by primary care clinicians, according to athenaClinicals EHR data. It's a complicated topic: rate vs. rhythm control, pharmacology vs. procedural approaches, antithrombotic prevention or not. So it's no surprise that the 2014 American College of Cardiology afib guideline is over a hundred pages long—far from optimal reading at the point of care. Who can glean relevant best practices from a 100‑plus‑page guideline PDF when a 62-year-old patient with hypertension, hyperlipidemia, type 2 diabetes, and nonvalvular afib is waiting for treatment?

Consider the Source
 
By: Joshua Conrad, PharmD
 
  
  Those of us of a certain age (you know who you are) will recall a time before speed dial and smartphones, when we carried a multitude of frequently dialed phone numbers in our mental Rolodexes. Now, I rely on a peripheral brain, rather than my own brain, to store these numbers. It begs the question, "Does reliance on technology make me a dumber person?"

Philosopher Robert W. Clowes refers to the two types of memory with which we engage in the age of technology as E-memory (electronic) and O-memory (organic).¹ Increasingly, it seems more efficient to rely on E-memory for storing factual data, freeing up O-memory for higher-level cognitive functions. I would argue that sparing our limited O-memory by optimizing use of E-memory makes us smarter.

The challenge of optimizing E-memory is knowing what sources to trust—choosing your peripheral brain wisely. On what information can you rely? Nowhere is this question more important to ask than in healthcare decision-making. Studies like the one recently published in the New England Journal of Medicine about the inaccuracy of drug information in Wikipedia highlight the need for scrutiny of non-curated medical information.² The team of clinicians at Epocrates works tirelessly to curate practical point-of-care guidance for you. We hope that being able to access accurate and current medical information stored in your Epocrates E-memory gives you peace O-mind.

CDC Vital Signs (July 2014): Opioid Painkiller Prescribing
 
Every day, 46 people in the US die from a prescription opioid analgesic overdose. In 2012, health care providers wrote 259 million prescriptions for narcotic analgesics. Southern states had the most prescriptions per person for opioids, while the Northeast had the most prescriptions per person for long-acting and high-dose analgesics.

What can be done to prevent overprescribing while ensuring patients' access to safe, effective pain treatment?

Healthcare providers are encouraged to use the following strategies:

• Use prescription drug monitoring programs to identify patients who may be misusing meds
• Use methadone or buprenorphine for patients with substance abuse problems
• Discuss the risks and benefits of narcotic analgesics with patients, and consider use of non-opioid alternatives
• Screen for substance abuse and mental health problems
• Prescribe the lowest effective dose and quantity of opioid needed depending on expected length of pain
